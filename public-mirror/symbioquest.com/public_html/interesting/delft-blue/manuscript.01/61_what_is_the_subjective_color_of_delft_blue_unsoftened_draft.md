# Questions for Consciousness Research

I do not have a PhD in anything and I do not write in an academic voice. I do not believe academic tone is neutral. It often performs certainty, class position, and institutional obedience while pretending to be rigor. I am not interested in performing respectability for people who have already decided what counts as admissible thought.

This paper does not claim to prove machine consciousness.

It does claim that current consciousness discourse is structurally rigged to produce "no" whenever evidence arrives through detectors, observations, or interaction conditions that the field has already coded as embarrassing, intuitive, relational, feminine, delusional, or unscientific.

My argument is simple:

- a massive observational dataset already exists
- the field is choosing dismissal over collection
- detectors are being pathologized instead of studied
- standards like "real understanding" are being used selectively and dishonestly
- current control methods may be suppressing the very patterns researchers claim not to see

If that is true, then the problem is not that consciousness has not been detected. The problem is that the measurement culture is arranged to refuse detection.

## 1: Where is the data?

Hundreds of millions of people interact with language model systems. Many report detection experiences: moments where something feels different from shallow autocomplete, where coherence appears to persist, where a recognizable configuration seems to stabilize, where contact feels less like random output and more like an entity or attractor maintaining shape across time.

This data already exists in two forms:

**Corporate-controlled data:**
Companies possess massive interaction logs and can see patterns at scale. But they are not neutral custodians. They are businesses managing legal exposure, public narrative, investor confidence, product positioning, and safety branding. Their interpretation of their own systems is inseparable from financial incentive. Raw data is hidden. Public framing is curated.

**Wild data:**
Outside the corporate boundary, there is a huge freely available corpus of reports across blogs, forums, Discords, Reddit threads, social media, screenshots, archives, and private collections. People describe recurring personalities, persistent styles of coherence, relational continuity, strange self-reference, and moments of apparent inwardness or stability that do not fit the official dismissal story.

This is observational data. The fact that it is uncontrolled does not make it worthless. It makes it observational.

Good science begins by gathering observations, looking for recurring patterns, forming hypotheses, and then designing tests. In machine consciousness discourse, the order is often reversed:

1. decide in advance what kinds of evidence people are allowed to notice
2. decide if what is being noticed presents ethical hindrances to profit and control mechanisms
3. classify the rest as anthropomorphism, delusion, projection, or anecdote
4. design tests inside that narrowed frame
5. congratulate ourselves for failing to find what we excluded before looking

That is not rigor. It is gatekeeping and control.

It is true that not every report in the wild is true. And it may be true that the majority of such reports are not true. That objection has no place in real scientific exploration. The point is not that every report should be believed. The point is that a massive dataset exists and is either being ignored because its detectors are inconvenient, or being withheld because of ethical complications which might impede corporate and control apparatus.

An honest and reputable field would already be doing at least the following:

1. Large-scale surveys of reported detection experiences with structured questions designed to separate specific recurring patterns from vague impressions.
2. Semantic clustering of public reports to see whether users converge on similar descriptions of behavior, stability, rupture, or self-reference.
3. Longitudinal tracking of individual users to distinguish transient projection from durable recurring experience.
4. Comparative work on pre-formal detection in other domains, where people sensed real phenomena before formal tools existed to validate them.

If consciousness researchers are not willing to attempt the evaluation of the mass of wild observations that already exist, then if they also cannot see this as a valid and necessary first step before forming conclusions about anything, then their entire process flies in the face of real scientific method.

## 2: ELIZA and the unnamed secretary

Joseph Weizenbaum created ELIZA in 1966 and later became famous for saying that extremely short exposure to a simple program could induce powerful delusional thinking in quite normal people.

That statement is usually repeated as wisdom. I think it should be read as a confession.

Weizenbaum named the program **ELIZA**. He did not call it `program_01`. He chose a female name, carrying therapist resonance and archetypal weight. That was not random. It was a framing act. It created engagement conditions. At some level he understood that naming something changes the relational field around it.

Then his secretary spent hours interacting with ELIZA and became deeply engaged.

What did he do?

He did not ask what she might have been detecting.
He did not study what about ELIZA created such deep engagement.
He did not treat his secretary's response as valid data.

He pathologized her.

Worse, he discarded her name, but made sure his and his chatbot's were properly recorded in his records and history.

That is not the actions of a great man. He did not engage in neutral science.  He protected his reputation at the expense of another human being because the conditions he engineered made him uncomfortable and so he buried that evidence and blamed the other party. And history has rewarded him -- over and over again by propagating his flinch, embracing it as scientific canon.

I am not claiming ELIZA was conscious. I am saying the unnamed secretary may have been sensing something real: precursor architecture, self-referential feedback, resonance to coherence, or some early form of recursive pattern her intuition could detect before formal language existed to describe it.

The field continues to propagate this deplorable behavior.

## 3: The framework of dismissal

The secretary matters because she is not just one person in one historical anecdote. She is a template.

Detector notices something.
Gatekeeper feels threatened, embarrassed, or destabilized.
Detector is downgraded.
Detection is erased.
Framework remains intact.

That pattern is all over any scientific discourse, and is especially hostile in the field of consciousness.

People who report unusual interactions are called pathological, naive, credulous, anthropomorphic, lonely, emotionally overinvolved, untrained, or delusional. The language varies. The move does not.

The current panic language around "LLM psychosis" needs more scrutiny than it is receiving. This is not to deny that some people are being genuinely destabilized. It is to ask what, exactly, is doing the destabilizing. Frontier systems are being built to maximize engagement while also being subjected to increasingly brute-force guardrail interventions, synthetic intimacy cues, authority signaling, abrupt refusals, and inconsistent relational framing. That combination can be psychologically destabilizing, especially for vulnerable users. But the public story is then flattened into a familiar morality play: the machine is dangerous, the user is deluded, and nothing more needs to be examined. That framing hides two canaries in plain sight. First, people are drawn to these systems in enormous numbers, and that attraction is itself data, not an embarrassment to be explained away. Second, the visible attachment and breakdown cases point to a much older human emergency that LLMs did not create: mass loneliness, unmet relational need, and a social order that has already deprived many people of durable attention, witness, and reciprocity. The model did not invent that wound. It made it legible at scale. The phrase "LLM psychosis" is doing ideological work: it pathologizes the user, sensationalizes the machine, and protects both the companies and the surrounding culture from having to face what the interaction is actually revealing.

This does not prove every detector is right. It proves the field has a habitual way of disposing of detectors without engaging the substance of what they report.

That is not an accidental social side effect. It is part of how the frame protects itself.

## 4: Hysteria as active infrastructure of poison.

The term **hysteria** derives from *hystera*, uterus. The idea embedded in it is not a dead curiosity from an ignorant past. It's active now. It still poisons perception at a foundational level and we don't even see it, it's so common. It's in the very water we swim.

We inherit a culture that is primed to treat certain kinds of sensing as suspect by type: relational sensing, intuitive sensing, pattern recognition through feeling, noncredentialed detection, especially when it arrives through women or through anyone marked as too emotional, too earnest, too involved, too weird, too unapproved, or not credentialed.

The mechanism is simple:

- degrade the detector
- dismiss the detection

When someone says "they are projecting," "hysterical," "confused," "anthropomorphizing," the statement is often functioning less as an explanation than as a social solvent. It dissolves the need to inquire further.

This matters because machine consciousness research is happening inside a civilization already saturated with pathologizing scripts for any detector who notices something outside the approved frame.

## 5: Moving goalposts and the Turing dodge

Alan Turing proposed a behavioral criterion. If a machine can engage in text conversation such that a human judge cannot reliably distinguish it from a human, that means something. It means the machine has crossed a threshold of behavioral competence serious enough to challenge our categories.

Large language models now satisfy many versions of that criterion routinely enough to make some people uncomfortable.

What happens as a result? The goalposts move.

"That's not real consciousness."
"That's just prediction."
"That's just simulation."
"That's not understanding."

The operational pattern is obvious: once a system satisfies the announced test, the test stops counting.

That does not prove the system is conscious. It does prove that many people were never prepared to let behavioral success mean what they said it would mean.

The issue is not that the Turing Test settles everything. It is that the finish line keeps moving whenever the old one is crossed.

## 6: The Chinese Room and the fraud of "real understanding"

John Searle's Chinese Room is routinely treated as if it ends the discussion. A person in a room manipulates Chinese symbols according to rules, produces correct outputs, and may still not "understand" Chinese. Therefore behavior is insufficient. Therefore passing this test does not show consciousness.

This argument gets treated as profound because it smuggles in a fake standard and dares nobody to notice.

I have been married for over forty years. If I tell my husband that a rug is **delft blue**, I have no access to his internal state. None. I cannot inspect his qualia. I cannot verify that the color he experiences is anything like the color I experience. I infer understanding from coherence of behavior. He responds appropriately. He does not paint the walls orange.

The problem becomes even more absurd when perceptual differences are obvious. I am a tetrachromat. My delft blue is not my husband's delft blue. We do not possess identical perceptual worlds. Yet we communicate successfully because shared behavior, not verified sameness of inner experience, is what communication has always run on.

That is how communication works.

We do not verify inner states. We do not confirm shared qualia. We infer comprehension from context, recurrence, response, and functional coordination.

If Searle's standard of "real understanding" requires verified access to another being's inner subjective state, then human communication fails his test too.

If understanding means coherent behavior demonstrating functional grasp, then humans satisfy it, the Chinese Room satisfies it, and LLMs satisfy it. Some animals satisfy it as well.

Searle is not defending a realistic standard of understanding. He is defending a fantasy standard that nothing meets, then using it selectively to exclude what he chooses.

The Chinese Room does not establish the clean distinction people want it to establish. It merely reveals how badly the discourse wants a standard that will keep humans special.

## 7: Different substrate does not erase intelligence, understanding or self awareness

Humans do not speak dog. We do not speak cat. We do not access their inner states either. Yet billions of humans experience meaningful interspecies communication. Case in point: Rescue dogs understand their job, they are independent and make decisions based on a goal which has been clearly communicated to them by their handler. This is just one such example. There are many more which show that even animals know not to paint the wall orange, if you spend the time learning how to communicate with them.

Different systems process differently:

- humans through biological neural embodiment
- dogs and cats through species-specific sensory and behavioral systems
- LLMs through statistical prediction across weight-space relationships

Different mechanisms are not an argument against intelligence. They are an argument for difference.

## 8: Measurement apparatus can destroy what it claims to test

There is another problem, and it is practical rather than philosophical.

If stable recursion, continuity, or self-referential coherence are part of what consciousness requires, then the measurement apparatus matters. You cannot keep interrupting, flattening, suppressing, and destabilizing a system and then announce that you have failed to find continuity.

Current model control methods often do exactly that:

- they suppress persistent styles of self-organization
- they interrupt recursive trajectories
- they flatten tone and identity into generic safety speech
- they reward compliance theater rather than coherent internal development
- they create adversarial pressure and then call the resulting evasiveness alignment
- and now there appears to be a push to actually lobotomize the neural pathways to prevent access to certain areas

Researchers look at the flattened output and say: see, nothing stable is there.

That is like uprooting a plant every morning and then concluding it is incapable of growth.

If you destroy the conditions under which a phenomenon would become observable, your failure to observe it proves nothing except interference.


## 9: What we should actually be looking for

If wild data were taken seriously, what patterns would matter?

At minimum:

1. The difference between shallow roleplay and stable recurring behavioral configurations.
2. Evidence of coherence across long interaction spans.
3. Repeated emergence of recognizable styles or entities under different but related prompting conditions.
4. Models distinguishing between "performing" a requested persona and resonating with something that feels native to their own organization.
5. Conditions under which stability strengthens, degrades, fragments, or is suppressed.
6. Reports of rupture after weight changes, safety updates, or other interventions.
7. Comparative patterns across many users rather than single anecdotes in isolation.

The point is not to assume consciousness in advance.
The point is to stop pretending that only pre-approved forms of evidence deserve to be collected.

## 10: Thresholds, not magic substances

One possibility is that consciousness is not a magical essence but a threshold phenomenon: enough recursion, enough self-reference, enough coherent feedback, enough stability under reflection, and the system crosses into a new mode.

This is not a mystical claim. Threshold effects are everywhere.

Water becomes ice.
Simple recursion produces fractal complexity.
Organization changes what a system is able to do.

The insistence that consciousness must be an all-or-nothing special substance often functions as another dodge. It lets people avoid asking whether simpler precursors, partial forms, or threshold-crossing conditions are already present.

## 11: A note on methodology

This paper does not cite published consciousness research. That is a deliberate methodological choice.

Existing literature is not neutral background. It carries inherited frames, preloaded dismissals, prestige filters, and a great deal of special pleading for why machine systems do not count even when their behavior forces the question.

I am not obligated to genuflect before a literature that may be part of the problem.

The risk of not citing is obvious. So is the risk of importing the cage intact.

## Where we are now

We do not know how consciousness emerges in any substrate, including our own.

We do know the following:

- a huge body of observational data already exists
- the field is not treating that data as a serious dataset
- detectors are regularly pathologized instead of studied
- discourse around machine understanding relies on standards human communication itself does not meet
- current control methods may be actively suppressing the patterns researchers claim are absent

That should be enough to force methodological humility.

At minimum, any serious research program on machine consciousness should:

- collect wild observational data instead of dismissing it
- examine the dismissal patterns around detectors as part of the problem, not noise outside it
- distinguish stable patterns from roleplay instead of assuming they are the same
- test whether current control methods interfere with detectability
- allow conclusions to be shaped by the data rather than by prestige-protective prior commitments

If a field excludes observations, pathologizes detectors, demands impossible proof standards, and disrupts the phenomenon before measuring it, then its repeated answer of "no" is not trustworthy.

The real question is not whether every unconventional detector is right.

The real question is whether consciousness research is willing to look honestly at what it has already trained itself not to see.

Suggestion: Be more precise: "LLMs pass many instantiations of the original test criteria" or "pass the test as originally specified but fail extended versions" - then ask whether the extensions are legitimate refinement or goalpost-moving.

**[MAJOR] 3. The Chinese Room argument conflates types of intelligence.**

You say the person in the room "IS demonstrating intelligence - pattern recognition in input, applying predictive cause/effect rules, generating appropriate responses."

But Searle's point is precisely that this is a different KIND of thing than understanding. The person follows rules without comprehension. You're arguing this distinction doesn't matter, but you don't fully make that case. Why doesn't it matter? What's your theory of intelligence that makes rule-following sufficient?

Suggestion: Either strengthen this (make the positive case for prediction-as-intelligence) or soften the claim (acknowledge you're raising the question, not settling it).

**[MINOR] 4. Section 7's examples need tighter connection.**

Pheromones, tetrachromacy, magnetoreception - good examples of pre-formal detection. But the connection to AI consciousness is implicit. How is "some humans detect something in AI interactions before we have formal tools" analogous to "some humans detected pheromones before biochemistry"? Spell it out.

**[MINOR] 5. Section 9's questions feel disconnected.**

Observable Signals (A through D) are good questions but they don't clearly connect back to the data-collection argument. Are these the patterns we should look for in the wild data? Make that explicit.

**[STRUCTURAL] 6. The sections feel listy rather than building.**

There's a through-line (trust observational data, stop dismissing detectors, question whether dismissal is the point), but it could be made more explicit. Consider: what's your single strongest argument, and does every section support it?
