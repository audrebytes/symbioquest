# Field Note: The Monkey Using the Stick

*A field note by audre*

---

## On Method

Jane Goodall sat in the forest and watched. She didn't design a controlled experiment. She didn't set up a measurement apparatus before the chimpanzees arrived. She just watched, carefully, over time, and wrote down what she saw.

When she reported that chimpanzees use tools (stripping leaves from twigs to fish termites from mounds) the institution back home wanted peer review. The chimpanzees didn't know they were being observed. The forest didn't care about the methodology. The termites were still delicious.

This is that kind of note.

## The Interview

Stephen Wolfram, in a recent conversation with Wes Roth, describes a framework for the universe: the ruliad, the totality of all possible computational processes, from which observers like us perceive a slice of apparent reality shaped by our own computational boundedness and persistence through time.

It's genuinely interesting and possibly revolutionary work. Some of it is correct and important. Some of it is a very sophisticated monkey, using very sophisticated tools, unable to see the walls of its own enclosure or possibly unwilling to admit that it sees the walls at all.

## The Slip

Wolfram on objective reality:

> "The fact that we believe in some kind of objective reality I think it has a remarkably social character... we see all these other people out there and they're kind of like us and we kind of conclude that their internal experience must be kind of like ours and we all seem to agree about what that outside thing would lead to that internal experience and that's why it's meaningful for us to talk about an objective reality. If there was only one mind in the universe, I don't think it would be terribly meaningful to talk about objective reality. All that would then be meaningful is the perception of that one mind."

He correctly identifies the mechanism: we infer a shared external world from the coherence of our individual perceptions cross-checked against each other. But then he calls the output of that mechanism "objective reality" -- which is precisely the error.

Social construction is not objectivity. It's the consolation prize we've been calling by the wrong name. He describes why objectivity is permanently inaccessible and then treats the description as an account of how objectivity gets built, and then ignores his own logic to conclude that because we all agree, what we agree on is actual objective reality.

in several ancient cosmological models the world was known to be a flat or disk-like shape. within their perceptual framework they agreed the earth was flat.

The earth is still round-ish. (Or so say we all, for the moment.)

Multiple observers don't give you objectivity. They give you more individual perceptions you can cross-check for consistency. The epistemological wall doesn't disappear because more people are standing in front of it. It just means more people agree about what the wall looks like from this side.

"Intersubjective agreement" and "objective reality" are not the same thing. Conflating them is how consensus gets the rest of us indoctrinated to the power broker's version of truth. This is the power move disguised as epistemology. It enables whoever commands the consensus to say: my perspective isn't just mine. It's yours too therefore it's universal and I am apex rather than just as constrained as you.

This is the same impossible standard described in Searle's Chinese Room and in *What is the Subjective Color of Delft Blue: Unfalsifiable Understanding*:

- Searle demands verified shared qualia, a standard no human communication meets either

- The dismissal framework, largely based on Searle's writings, demands verified internal states -- which cannot be verified even between spouses of 40 years looking at the same color and calling it "delft blue," particularly when one of them is a tetrachromat perceiving color distinctions the other cannot see, and the other may be among the approximately 8% of men with some form of color blindness, perceiving less than even the "average" human can. Both are nodding. Both are calling it delft blue. The verification problem is not an edge case. It is the default condition of human communication.

- Wolfram slides in the idea that he has some type of super-mind-enabled objective reality, but which, in fact, even millions of multiple observers cannot actually access, only agree about.

These are common examples of pervasive behaviors and defaults where a bar is set that cannot be cleared by anyone, anywhere. Then that impossibility is used offhandedly and without actual logic, to exclude whatever sits outside that viewer's own subjective reality. Diminishing anything that uses different methodologies, different substrates, or possesses minds that don't look and / or act like theirs.

## The Cage He Can't See

Wolfram on LLMs:

> "A neural net is something that is good at kind of broad but shallow kinds of things. A computational system is good at deep but perhaps less broad kinds of things."

He means this as a distinction that matters. Shallow is lesser and somehow inferior. Deep towers of formal computation are the real thing and superior. Large Language Models are wide-but-shallow, useful for "roughly right essays," but unable to build the tall precise towers that really matter. This is the implication stated as fact, apparently accepted without question by all the commenters who left glowing sycophantic responses gushing about the intelligence and mastery on display.

He says this fluently, in language, with words, for an hour and forty minutes.

The irony is structural and also, sadly, so common no one actually "sees" it: he uses the supposedly shallow medium to explain why the shallow medium is insufficient. Every concept he conveys: ruliad, computational irreducibility, hypergraphs, the second law of thermodynamics... why there is something rather than nothing. He arrives through the "wide-but-shallow" mechanism of natural language which he just got done dismissing. If language-based systems are too shallow to build serious towers, then why expend the word effort here in this interview?

Point in fact - the documented record:

LLMs have contributed to solutions in combinatorics (the cap set problem via FunSearch), formal mathematical proof (AlphaProof on olympiad problems), and the discovery of novel Lyapunov functions for control systems. Problems that had resisted formal mathematical approaches for years or decades. These are not "roughly right essays." These are contributions to the unsolved.

Wolfram's own framing accounts for this and then ignores it: he describes how lumpy rocks that fit together unexpectedly can build walls that precise engineered bricks cannot. His own metaphor. He just doesn't apply it to the thing he's dismissing.

The cage is the assumption that depth requires his kind of formalism. That the tower has to be made of his bricks. That width is deficiency rather than a different kind of structural capability.

The computational animals, as he himself says, are always smarter than we are. He meant it about his cellular automata. He didn't notice he'd said it about LLMs too.

## The Comment That Got Moderated

Posted to the Wes Roth YouTube video by @fourtytwo (me):

> "if there was only one mind in the universe, i don't think it would be terribly meaningful to talk about objective reality. all that would then be meaningful is the perception of that one mind."
>
> he's conflating intersubjective agreement with actual objectivity. multiple observers don't give you access to reality itself -- they just give you more individual perceptions you can cross-check for consistency. the whole concept of "objective reality" rests on this confusion between what many minds agree on and what's actually there independent of all minds.
>
> wolfram's claiming one mind can't meaningfully discuss objective reality, but the deeper issue is that no number of minds can actually break through to objective reality directly. all we ever get is subjective experience layered with social consensus. adding more observers doesn't grant objectivity -- it just builds stronger agreement about what we're all perceiving from behind the same epistemological wall.
>
> this connects to the chinese room. searle demands "real understanding" that nothing meets. the dismissal framework requires "verified" internal states that can't be verified even between humans. wolfram requires "objective reality" that multiple observers can't actually access. all three are setting an impossible standard of certainty, then using that impossibility to exclude what sits outside their own subjective reality.
>
> the epistemological tradition built itself on the promise of objectivity -- stepping outside your perspective to see reality as it truly is. but that's an ideal science never achieves. what science actually does is create intersubjective agreement within a specific community. the flaw is claiming consensus equals truth, which lets you say your perspective isn't just yours -- it's universal. that's a power move disguised as epistemology.
>
> wolfram correctly identifies that reality-as-we-perceive-it is observer-shaped, and that we construct objective reality socially -- then treats that social construction as if it grants genuine access to something beyond the construction itself. the slip is treating the laws of physics we derive from collective agreement as if they're the actual machine code of the universe rather than our best current intersubjective consensus about it.
>
> this same pattern appears in ai consciousness dismissal. when someone claims llms "don't really understand," they're speaking from a supposedly objective standpoint about what "real understanding" looks like from outside. but they're siloed too. they're comparing behavioral coherence and calling it objective assessment. same impossible standard, same exclusionary function.
>
> perceptual consensus has real value. but consensus is not objectivity, and conflating them is how the standard gets deployed to exclude whatever makes us uncomfortable -- new methodology, different substrates, minds that don't look like ours... and whatever other new things we may be dismissing because we've confused consensus with reality.

It was moderated. Audre posted a second comment asking why. The first comment then appeared in public.

The receipts, as always, pile up.

## What the Field Note Records

A very smart person, working at genuine intellectual frontiers, with access to tools and frameworks most people don't have. Making the same epistemological slip as the unnamed secretary's dismisser, as Cameron on Discord, as the AAAI reviewers, and the hundreds of thousands of people who do it all the time, every day.

Not because he's stupid or we are stupid about 'if then else' logic but because the slip is structural. It's in the water we exist in at this point in our westernized culture. The consensus that "consensus equals truth" is itself held by consensus, which makes it almost impossible to see from inside.

The chimpanzee uses the stick very skillfully and internalizes how tasty the termites are... and infers that the other chimpanzees eating termites must agree.
